Justified True Belief:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is sufficient that they believe the proposition, have justification for believing the proposition, and the proposition is true

Reliabilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is produced by a reliable cognitive process
- A reliable cognitive process is one that produces a high proportion of true beliefs over false ones

No False Lemmas:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is not based on any false assumptions or lemmas

Causal Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there is an appropriate causal connection between the fact that makes the proposition true and the person's belief in the proposition

Virtue Epistemology:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is the result of exercising intellectual virtue
- An intellectual virtue is a trait that contributes to the acquisition of true beliefs

Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- The standards for justification can vary depending on the context in which the knowledge claim is made

Infinitism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- Justification for a belief requires an infinite chain of reasons

Coherentism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief coheres with a system of interconnected beliefs
- Coherence involves logical consistency and mutual support among beliefs

Foundationalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is either foundational or derived from foundational beliefs
- Foundational beliefs are self-evident, infallible, or evident to the senses

Pragmatic Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief works or is useful in practical terms
- Practical utility is determined by the success of actions based on the belief

Defeasibility Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there are no defeaters for their justification

Externalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is formed through a process that is externally reliable
- External reliability does not require the person to have access to or awareness of the reliability of the process

Internalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- Justification must be accessible to the person in order for them to have knowledge

Truth-Tracking Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief tracks the truth across close possible worlds
- Truth-tracking involves believing the proposition when it is true and not believing it when it is false in nearby possible situations

Epistemic Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- The truth conditions of knowledge attributions can change depending on the context of the speaker and the attributor

Knowledge First Theory:
- Knowledge is a fundamental concept that cannot be analyzed in terms of other concepts like belief or justification
- Knowledge is a starting point for understanding other epistemic concepts
- Belief and justification are to be understood in terms of their relation to knowledge

Phenomenal Conservatism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that it seems to them that the proposition is true
- Seemings provide prima facie justification for beliefs unless there are defeaters

Evidentialism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is supported by evidence
- Evidence consists of information or data that increases the likelihood of the truth of the proposition

Infinitism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- Justification for a belief requires an infinite chain of reasons

Disjunctivism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- Perceptual knowledge is either a direct acquaintance with the fact or a mere appearance without the fact
- In cases of knowledge, the experience is fundamentally different from cases of mere belief
