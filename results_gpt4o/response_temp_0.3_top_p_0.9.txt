Justified True Belief Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is sufficient that they believe the proposition, have justification for believing the proposition, and the proposition is true

Reliabilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief in the proposition is produced by a reliable cognitive process
- A reliable cognitive process is one that produces a high proportion of true beliefs over false ones

No False Lemmas Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is not based on any false lemmas
- A lemma is an intermediate proposition used in reasoning to support a conclusion

Causal Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there is an appropriate causal connection between the fact that makes the proposition true and the person's belief in the proposition

Virtue Epistemology:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is the result of exercising intellectual virtue
- An intellectual virtue is a character trait that reliably leads to truth

Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- The standards for justification can vary depending on the context in which the knowledge claim is made

Pragmatic Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is useful or beneficial in a practical sense
- The practical utility of a belief is determined by its ability to guide successful action

Infinitism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- Justification for a belief consists of an infinite chain of reasons

Coherentism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief coheres with a system of interconnected beliefs
- Coherence involves logical consistency and mutual support among beliefs

Foundationalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is either foundational or derived from foundational beliefs
- Foundational beliefs are self-evident, infallible, or evident to the senses

Defeasibility Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there are no defeaters for their justification
- A defeater is a truth that, if known, would undermine or counteract the justification for the belief

Truth-Tracking Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief tracks the truth
- A belief tracks the truth if, in nearby possible worlds where the proposition is false, the person does not believe it, and in nearby possible worlds where the proposition is true, the person believes it

Phenomenal Conservatism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that it seems to the person that the proposition is true
- Seemings provide prima facie justification for beliefs unless there are defeaters

Evidentialism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is supported by evidence
- Evidence consists of information or data that increases the likelihood of the proposition being true

Externalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is not necessary that the person has access to the justification for their belief
- Justification can depend on factors external to the person's mental states

Internalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the person has access to the justification for their belief
- Justification depends solely on factors internal to the person's mental states

Constructivism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is constructed through rational deliberation and social interaction
- Knowledge is a product of cognitive and social processes

Fallibilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is not necessary that their justification guarantees the truth of the proposition
- Knowledge can be based on justification that is not infallible

Infallibilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their justification guarantees the truth of the proposition
- Knowledge requires infallible justification

Reductionism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief can be reduced to more basic beliefs or experiences
- Knowledge is analyzable in terms of simpler components

Non-Reductionism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is not necessary that their belief can be reduced to more basic beliefs or experiences
- Knowledge cannot always be analyzed in terms of simpler components
