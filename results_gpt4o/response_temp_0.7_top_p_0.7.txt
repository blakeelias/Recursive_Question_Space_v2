Justified True Belief Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is sufficient that they believe the proposition, have justification for believing the proposition, and the proposition is true

Reliabilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is produced by a reliable cognitive process
- A reliable cognitive process is one that produces a high percentage of true beliefs

No False Lemmas Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is not based on any false assumptions or lemmas

Causal Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there is an appropriate causal connection between the belief and the fact that makes the proposition true

Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the standards for justification are met within the relevant context
- The standards for justification can vary depending on the context in which the knowledge claim is made

Virtue Epistemology:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief arises from the exercise of intellectual virtue
- An intellectual virtue is a character trait that reliably leads to the acquisition of true beliefs

Infinitism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their justification consists of an infinite chain of reasons

Coherentism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is part of a coherent system of beliefs
- A coherent system of beliefs is one in which the beliefs support each other

Foundationalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief is either foundational or derived from foundational beliefs
- Foundational beliefs are self-evident or evident to the senses

Pragmatism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief works or is useful in practical terms
- A belief is considered true if it proves to be useful and successful in the believer's experiences

Defeasibility Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there are no defeaters for the belief
- A defeater is a truth that, if known, would undermine the justification for the belief

Truth-Tracking Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that their belief tracks the truth
- A belief tracks the truth if it changes appropriately with changes in the truth of the proposition

Epistemic Externalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the factors contributing to the justification of the belief can be external to the believer's mental states

Epistemic Internalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the justification for the belief is accessible to the believer's consciousness

Subject-Sensitive Invariantism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the justification for the belief is sensitive to the subject's practical interests
- The truth conditions for knowledge do not vary with different contexts

Knowledge First Theory:
- Knowledge is a fundamental concept that cannot be analyzed in terms of other concepts
- Knowledge is more basic than belief or justification
- Other epistemic concepts should be understood in terms of knowledge
