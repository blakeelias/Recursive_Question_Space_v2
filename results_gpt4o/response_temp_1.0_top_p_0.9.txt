Traditional Tripartite Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary for the proposition to be true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is sufficient that they believe the proposition, have justification for believing the proposition, and the proposition is true

Reliabilism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is produced by a reliable cognitive process
- A reliable cognitive process is one that produces a high ratio of true beliefs to false beliefs

No False Lemmas Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they have justification for believing the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is not inferred from any falsehoods

Causal Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there is an appropriate causal connection between the belief and the fact

Virtue Epistemology:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief arises from the exercise of intellectual virtue
- An intellectual virtue is a trait that contributes to acquiring true beliefs

Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the person meets the relevant standards of justification
- The relevant standards of justification vary with the context

Infinitism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is justified by an infinite chain of reasons

Coherentism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief coheres with a system of beliefs

Foundationalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is either basic or derived from basic beliefs
- Basic beliefs are self-evident, infallible, or evident to the senses

Externalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there is an appropriate external relation to the fact

Internalism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the person has reflective access to the justification of the belief

Pragmatic Theory of Knowledge:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is useful or successful in action

Defeasibility Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there are no defeaters for the belief
- A defeater is a true proposition that, if known, would undermine the justification for the belief

Truth-Tracking Theory:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the person would not believe the proposition if it were false
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the person would believe the proposition if it were true

Epistemic Contextualism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- The standards for justification vary depending on the epistemic context of the conversation

Evidentialism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is justified by evidence
- Evidence consists of all the information available to the person

Phenomenal Conservatism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief seems true to the person
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that there are no defeaters for the belief seeming true

Modal Epistemology:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is safe
- A belief is safe if, in nearby possible worlds where the person forms the belief in the same way, the belief is true

Constructivism:
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that they believe the proposition
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the proposition is true
- Knowledge is constructed by social processes and shared within a community
- For a person to know a proposition, it is necessary that the belief is accepted by the community as justified
